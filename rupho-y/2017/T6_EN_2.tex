\section*{Part A. Modification of Newtonian dynamics}

The dependence of the orbital speed of stars and gas in a galaxy on the distance
to the center of the galaxy is called the rotation curve of the galaxy.
According to Newtonian dynamics, the speed should decrease with distance from the
center of the galaxy (Fig.~1A), assuming that most of the galaxy's mass is located
at its center. However, it turns out that in fact the speed is an almost constant
function of the radius (Fig.~1B). One solution to this discrepancy can be obtained
by modifying Newtonian dynamics as follows.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{T6_EN_assets/fig1.jpg}
    \caption{Figure 1.}
\end{figure}

Acceleration due to gravity depends not just on the mass $m$, but on
$m\cdot{\mu\!\left(\frac{a}{a_0}\right)}$, where $\mu(x)$ is a certain function
whose value tends to unity for large arguments ($x\gg{1}$) and to $x$ for small
arguments ($x\ll 1$), $a$ is the acceleration due to gravity, and $a_0$ is a
constant. Thus, Newton's second law takes the form:
$F=m\cdot{\mu\!\left(\frac{a}{a_0}\right)a}$.
The centripetal acceleration of stars and gas clouds on the outskirts of spiral
galaxies is, as observed, much less than $a_0$.
A graph of the actually measured rotation curve of the Milky Way is shown in
Figure~2. Here $1~\text{kpc} = 1~\text{kiloparsec}$ and
$1~\text{parsec} = 3\cdot{10^{16}}~\text{m}$.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{T6_EN_assets/fig2.jpg}
    \caption{Figure 2.}
\end{figure}

\problembox{A1}{How does the orbital rotation speed depend on the distance to the
galactic nucleus in the case of ordinary Newtonian dynamics?
Consider that the mass of the galaxy is concentrated in its core.}{0.50}

\problembox{A2}{Let in reality the orbital rotation speed be constant $v_\infty$
at large distances from the galactic center (as shown by the measured curve in
Fig.~1B). Find the centripetal acceleration $a(r)$ of a star at distance $r$
from the galactic center. Estimate $v_\infty$ from the graph and compute $a$ at
$r=10~\text{kpc}$. Compare this with the constant $a_0$ and discuss whether the
MOND regime ($a\ll a_0$) is applicable at these distances.}{1.00}

\problembox{A3}{Using the portion of the curve in the region $r<1~\text{parsec}$,
estimate the mass of the Milky Way core, assuming that Newtonian dynamics operates
near the core at distances $r<1~\text{parsec}$ from the galactic center.
Assume that the main contribution to gravity is made only by the mass in
the core.}{1.00}

\problembox{A4}{Taking into account the modified Newtonian dynamics described
above, prove that at large distances from the galactic nucleus, where the
acceleration due to gravity is small, the orbital speed does not depend on the
distance to the center and is equal to a constant. Let this speed far from the
center be equal to $v_\infty$. How are the constants $a_0$ and $v_\infty$
related? Using the graph, estimate $v_\infty$.}{1.00}

\problembox{A5}{Estimate the value of the constant $a_0$ based on the data in
the graph, assuming that in reality the mass of the entire Milky Way is
$k=10^6$ times greater than the mass of the core.}{0.50}

\section*{Part B. Tidal forces}

\problembox{B1}{It is known that the power of tidal frictional forces acting on
the Earth, caused by the Moon's revolution around it, is $N\approx 10^{12}~\text{W}$.
Estimate, by order of magnitude, the increment in the duration of the Earth's
day $\Delta T_\text{E}$ and the lunar month $\Delta T_\text{l}$ over a time
$\tau=100~\text{million years}$. The average radius of the Moon's orbit is
$R=4\cdot{10^5}~\text{km}$, the mass of the Moon is $m_\text{M}=10^{22}~\text{kg}$,
the orbital period of the Moon is $T_\text{M}=27.3~\text{days}$, and the moment
of inertia of the Earth is $I=8\cdot{10^{37}}~\text{kg\,m}^2$.
The orbital motion of the Moon is co-directed with the rotation of the
Earth around its axis.}{3.00}

\section*{Part C. Relativistic processes}

\problembox{C1}{The relativistic $\pi^0$-meson decayed into two gamma quanta with
energies $E_1=81~\text{MeV}$ and $E_2=450~\text{MeV}$. Find the angle $\varphi$
between the directions of the two gamma quanta.
The rest mass of the $\pi^0$-meson is $135~\text{MeV}/c^2$.}{1.00}

\problembox{C2}{A positron with Lorentz factor $\gamma=3$ collides with an
electron at rest. The resulting two gamma quanta scatter symmetrically relative
to the direction of the positron. Find the angle $\varphi$ between the directions
of the two gamma quanta.}{2.00}
