D. Dielectric constant of non-polar liquids

In order to determine the dielectric constant of liquids, it is no longer possible to assume that the strengths $\vec{E}$ and $\vec{E}'$ (see C3) coincide. To calculate $\vec{E}'$, we can assume that the molecule is inside a hollow empty (without matter) sphere in the dielectric material. To find the field inside this sphere created by external charges, we assume that these external charges are distributed continuously in the substance.

E. Dielectric constant of polar substances

For polar dielectrics the situation is more complicated. The molecules themselves have a dipole moment $p_0$. In the absence of an electric field, these dipole molecules are randomly oriented. When this substance is placed in an electric field, polarization appears due to the uneven orientation of the dipoles. In addition, there remains the effect that in addition to the own dipole moment, an induced one appears. The contribution of these two effects to the dielectric constant adds linearly.

E1
Determine the difference $\varepsilon-1$ for such a substance with the concentration of molecules $n$ at temperature $T$. We can assume that the temperature is quite high, but the electric field strength is not high. Ignore the induced dipole moment.
1.00
