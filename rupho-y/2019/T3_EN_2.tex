The magnetic separation method is used to separate materials of different magnetic properties. For example, titanium ore deposits contain minerals such as zircon ($\rm ZrSiO_4$), monazite ($\rm \left[Ce{,}La{,}Th\right]PO_4$), ilmenite ($\rm FeTiO_3$), rutile ($\rm TiO_2$), among which the economic importance of titanium-containing minerals is difficult to overestimate. Rutile and ilmenite are used in the production of non-toxic pigments used in inks, plastics, ceramics and more. Therefore, rutile and ilmenite must be separated from titanium ores. Magnetic separation uses magnetic forces to separate magnetic materials. The operating principle of the magnetic separator is shown in Fig. 1.

Fig. 1: (1) box; (2) non-magnetic roller $L_1$; (3) magnetic roller $L_2$; (4) conveyor belt; (5) magnetic particles; (6) collection trays.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{T3_EN_assets/fig1.jpg}
    \caption{Figure 1.}
\end{figure}

The main part of the magnetic separator consists of two rollers $L_1$ and $L_2$ (diameter $D$), connected by a conveyor belt (Fig. 1). The thickness of the tape compared to the radius of the rollers can be neglected. Roller $L_1$ is non-magnetic. The roller $L_2$ is magnetic, and the magnetic field around it changes according to the following law $$B(r) = \left|\vec{B}\right| = B_0 e^{-r/a},$$ where $r$ is the distance from the surface of the roller in the radial direction, and $a$ is some constant. The magnetic roller rotates with angular velocity $\omega$, which is maintained by the electric motor. Mineral particles flow from the box onto a conveyor belt, and along it to the magnetic roller. The friction between the belt and the magnetic roller is high enough to prevent slipping between them.

The following mathematical formulas may be useful: $$\coth{x}=\cfrac{e^x+e^{-x}}{e^x-e^{-x}}= \begin{cases} 1+e^{-2x}\quad\text{for}\quad x\to{\infty}\\ \cfrac{1}{x}+\cfrac{x}{3}-\cfrac{x^3}{45}\quad\text{for}\quad x\to{0} \end{cases}, $$$$e^x=\sum\limits_{n=0}^{\infty}\cfrac{x^n}{n!}\approx{1+\cfrac{x}{1!}+\cfrac{x^2}{2!}+\cfrac{x^3}{3!}+{...}} $$

In this part we will discuss the model of this magnetic separator, taking into account only magnetic and gravitational forces.

\section*{Part A. Magnetic susceptibility of materials}

Let's study the properties of paramagnetic materials. Let us consider a mineral consisting of magnetic atoms $X$ with concentration $n$, each atom has a magnetic moment $p$. The concentration is low, so the interaction of the magnetic moments of atoms with each other can be neglected. In the absence of an external magnetic field, the magnetic moments of the atoms are directed randomly, resulting in the magnetization $\vec{M}$ being zero. The magnetization of a substance is the magnetic moment of a unit volume of a substance. In an external magnetic field, atoms are ordered along the direction of the external magnetic field $\vec{B}$, a magnetic moment appears in the material, and non-zero magnetization is obtained. According to quantum mechanics, the projection of the magnetic moment of an atom onto the field direction (we choose the $z$ axis along it) is equal to: $$p_z = g_j\mu_B m,$$ where $m$ is a number that takes values in the set $\{j, j-1, j-2, \ldots, -j\}$, $j$ is a non-negative integer or half-integer characterizing the atom $X$, $\mu_B = 0.93\cdot 10^{-23}~\text{A}\cdot\text{m}^2$ is the Bohr magneton, and $g_j$ is the so-called Land\'e factor. The probability $W(p_z)$ that the projection of the magnetic moment on the field direction takes the value $p_z$ is given by $$W(p_z) = A\, e^{-\frac{U_B}{kT}},$$ where $A$ is a normalization constant, $U_B$ is the energy of the atom in the field, $T$ is the temperature, and $k$ is Boltzmanns constant. Note that, by symmetry, the average projections of the magnetic moment on the $x$ and $y$ axes are equal to zero.

\problembox{A1}{Find the average value $\langle p_z\rangle$ of the component of the magnetic moment of an atom $X$ on the axis $z$ located in the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ at temperature $T$, as a function of the parameter $\alpha=\frac{g_j\mu_BB}{kT}$.}{1.10}

\problembox{A2}{Give an expression for $\langle p_z\rangle$ in the limiting case $\alpha\to{0}$ (that is, for cases where the magnetic energy is much less than the thermal energy). Consider also the case $\alpha\to{\infty}$. Provide a schematic graph of the dependence of $\langle p_z\rangle$ on the parameter $\alpha$.}{1.10}

Magnetic susceptibility $\chi$ characterizes the magnetic properties of a substance. In a weak magnetic field $\vec{B}$ the susceptibility is defined as $\chi=\frac{\mu_0M}{B}$, where $M$ magnetization, $\mu_0$ is the magnetic permeability of the vacuum. Consider the magnetic field weak, that is, the parameter $\alpha\ll{1}$.

\problembox{A3}{Calculate the magnetic susceptibility $\chi$. Express your answer in terms of atomic concentration $n$, temperature $T$, Lande factor $g_j$, parameter $j$, and fundamental constants.}{0.90}
