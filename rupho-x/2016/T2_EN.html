Cooling of non-ideal gases

X16 (2016) — English translation

For real gases, the Mendeleev-Clapeyron equation $pV=\nu RT$ is satisfied approximately and the more accurately, the lower the density of the gas and the higher its temperature. In real gases, forces act between molecules, and at close distances these are repulsive forces, and at large distances they are attractive. One of the simple equations that takes into account the interaction of molecules is the Van der Waals equation of state: $$\left(p+\frac{a\nu^2}{V^2}\right)\left(V-\nu b\right)=RT $$ Где $a$ и $b$ — положительные коэффициенты Ван-дер-Ваальса, которые в этой задаче можно считать постоянными. Член $\frac{a\nu^2}{V^2}$ описывает уменьшение давления газа из-за взаимного притяжения молекул, а $\nu b$ - takes into account the finite size of the molecules. Note that the van der Waals equation of state can qualitatively describe both the behavior of a gas and the behavior of a liquid.

The Van der Waals equation of state is a third degree equation in coordinates ($p$,$V$). In Fig. Figure 1 shows a series of isotherms of one mole of van der Waals gas. The real isotherm differs from the van der Waals isotherm by a straight section $AB$ with constant pressure $p_{LG}$, located along the volume axis between $V_L$ and $V_G$, on which the equilibrium of liquid (indicated by the index $L$) and gas (indicated by the index $G$) is realized. Using the second law of thermodynamics, J. Maxwell showed that the pressure $p_{LG}$ should be chosen in such a way that those shown in Fig. 2 areas I and II were the same. Also, the internal energy of a van der Waals gas depends not only on temperature, but also on volume: $$U=\nu C_V T-\frac{a\nu^2}{V} $$

A1
One of the industrial methods of cooling (and subsequent liquefaction) is the adiabatic and quasi-static expansion of gases. Let the gas expand from the initial volume $V_1$ to the final volume $V_2$. For a gas that obeys the van der Waals equation of state, in this process the temperature drops by $\Delta{T}_W$, and for an ideal gas - $\Delta{T}_I.$ The heat capacity $C_V$ of a van der Waals gas is taken equal to the heat capacity of an ideal gas $C_V$ at constant volume. Find the ratio $\Delta{T}_W/\Delta{T}_I$. Express your answer in terms of $V_1$, $V_2$, $a$, $b$, $C_V$ and $R$. In which case is there more cooling?
